Why do some states like Hawaii have dramatically
higher percentages?

Though they make one United States of America, each state has its own
unique history. Hawaii is an exceptionally unique case. In undergrad, I
learned that due to its proximity to Asia, Hawaii hosted many immigrants from Asia
who intermarried with each other and native Hawaiians. In fact, in the 1960s,
anthropologists studied the people of Hawaii idealizing the strength and attractive-
ness of mixed race folks on the islands, which might be considered today to be
idealized, reductionist classifications. The Hawaiian term "hapa" means "mixed race"
has been adopted into English American vernacular today.


What exactly is mixed race?

Fasciatingly, most racial categories in the US census have not been contsant
throughout the country's history. For example, in the cirst census in 1870, the closest
categorization to what today is understood as "mixed race" was "mulatto", which meant
black and some other race. Other cateogires in that original census were: White, Black,
Indian, and Chinese. Meanwhile, come 1920, census race categories were the original
original categories plus Japanese, Filipino, Korean, and Hindu. Today, the race categories
offered are even more different. For more information, you can go this interesting page
by the Pew Research Center:

http://www.pewsocialtrends.org/interactives/multiracial-timeline/

It is interesting to consider the data model used by US census creators
and the political and social goals (use goals?) behind them. Is it a structured
dataset since the census comes up with the model? Or is it a semistructured set since
people fill out which racial category to which they belong on their census forms?

It is also worthwhile considering the human agency and inconsistency in census racial
categorization. For instance, a person in 1870 may have been Chinese in the eyes of
the US government, but come 1920, that same person may have been Filipino.
